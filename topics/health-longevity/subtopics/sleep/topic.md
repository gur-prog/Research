# health-longevity / sleep — Topic Scope

## Inherited follow-up goal

Change my habits and routines (workout, eating, sleeping, working, etc.) in a way that is healthy and would support longevity, considering my career goals (I aspire to be an electrical engineer).

## Subtopic goal

The parent research established that 7–8 hours is a U-shaped optimum for cognitive decline risk ([fact:28–31] in parent) — but that finding left four practical questions unanswered that matter for daily decisions: is the optimal window universal or per-person, is longer sleep genuinely harmful or a disease marker, how do I self-assess sleep quality without a lab, and what actually works to improve it. This subtopic is the drill-down on all four.

## Research questions

1. **Is the "optimal sleep window" subjective or universal?** Are the 7–8-hour population findings the same answer for every individual, or does chronotype, age, genetics, or context meaningfully shift the right target? What's the evidence base for individual variation — specifically on genetic short-sleepers (DEC2/ADRB1 variants) and on within-person need stability.

2. **What are the effects of too-long sleep (>9 hours)?** Parent facts flagged higher dementia risk at long sleep but suggested reverse causation. Resolve the causal question as far as current evidence allows: is long sleep itself harmful, a symptom of something else, or confounded by underlying disease / depression / fragmented sleep time in bed?

3. **How can I tell if I'm sleeping well without a sleep lab?** What are the validated self-assessment indicators — subjective (PSQI-style items, morning refreshedness, daytime sleepiness) and behavioral (sleep latency, wakeups, consistent wake time, weekend rebound)? How reliable are consumer wearables (Oura/Whoop/Apple Watch) for sleep staging and quality estimation?

4. **What actually improves sleep quality?** Evidence-based interventions ranked by effect size: sleep hygiene fundamentals (wake-time consistency, light exposure, temperature, caffeine timing), CBT-I, exercise timing, diet/timing of eating, supplements (melatonin, magnesium), and common traps (alcohol as a sleep aid, screens-in-bed). Focus on what a healthy student can implement without clinical support.

## Scope in

- Healthy adults, especially young adults and students
- Individual variation in sleep need
- Self-assessment without clinical instruments
- Behavioral and environmental interventions
- Consumer wearable validation (briefly — as an assessment tool, not a product review)

## Scope out

- Clinical sleep disorders (OSA, narcolepsy, RLS) — referral territory, not habit-change territory. The parent already notes sleep-disordered breathing carries the biggest vascular dementia risk.
- Pharmacological sleep aids (prescription)
- Pediatric and elderly populations
- Shift work (not relevant to user's current context)

## Credibility filters

Same as parent: prefer peer-reviewed meta-analyses, large cohorts, and guideline consensus. For wearable validation specifically, peer-reviewed validation studies trump manufacturer claims.
