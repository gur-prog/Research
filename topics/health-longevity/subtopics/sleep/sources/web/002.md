# Long Sleep Duration and Dementia — Meta-Analysis

## Why this source

2025 systematic review and meta-analysis published in Journals of Gerontology that directly addresses Q2: is long sleep (>8h) actually harmful, a disease marker, or confounded? Explicitly takes on reverse causation and concludes causation remains unclear.

## Content

### Effect sizes for long sleep (>8h) vs 7–8h reference

- **Dementia:** HR 1.29 (95% CrI 0.94–1.77), posterior probability 94.5% that HR > 1 → ~29% increased risk
- **Cognitive decline:** RR 1.13 (95% CI 0.92–1.40) — not statistically significant
- **Absolute dementia-free time lost:** 0.13 years fewer by year 20 from age 65

### Reverse causation assessment

Authors acknowledge the concern directly: "long sleep duration might be a prodromal symptom of dementia." They cite three partial defenses against reverse causation:

1. All included studies excluded people with cognitive impairment at baseline
2. 5 of 6 studies had long follow-ups (7–16.6 years)
3. Cumulative incidence curves don't diverge until late in follow-up

### Authors' causal verdict

> It remains unclear whether these are independent risk factors or merely reflect the same underlying pathological process.

They propose long sleep may indicate **poor sleep quality** (time in bed ≠ actual sleep) rather than sleep duration being causal, and suggest future work reframe the question as "disordered sleep" rather than duration.
