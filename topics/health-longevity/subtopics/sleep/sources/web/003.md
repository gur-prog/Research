# Wearable Sleep Tracker Validation (Oura, Fitbit, Apple Watch)

## Why this source

Head-to-head validation of three consumer wearables vs. polysomnography in 35 healthy adults — answers Q3 on whether consumer wearables are reliable enough to tell if you sleep well.

## Content

### Sleep vs. wake detection (all devices excellent)

- **Oura Ring:** 95% sensitivity
- **Fitbit Sense 2:** 95% sensitivity
- **Apple Watch Series 8:** 97% sensitivity

> All three devices perform extremely well in distinguishing between sleep and wake.

### Total sleep duration accuracy

- **Oura:** no significant difference from PSG (421 min vs. PSG 430 min)
- **Fitbit:** systematic bias — overestimates light sleep by 18 min, underestimates deep sleep by 15 min
- **Apple Watch:** significant errors — underestimates deep sleep by 43 min, overestimates light sleep by 45 min

### Sleep stage detection (epoch-by-epoch)

- **Oura:** 76.0–79.5% sensitivity across stages (most consistent)
- **Fitbit:** 61.7–78.0% sensitivity
- **Apple Watch:** 50.5–86.1% sensitivity (most variable)

### Practical limitations

- Single-night lab study; longitudinal home accuracy unknown
- Device failures: Fitbit failed for 2/35, Apple Watch failed for 6/35 participants
- Algorithm opacity — no access to raw data or scoring logic
- Cost: $250–$800

## Notable quotes

> All three devices perform extremely well in distinguishing between sleep and wake.

> Wearables recommend future research focus on populations with disrupted sleep rather than only healthy sleepers.
