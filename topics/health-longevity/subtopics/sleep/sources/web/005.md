# Measuring Subjective Sleep Quality — PSQI Review

## Why this source

Review covering subjective sleep-quality self-assessment tools, their reliability, and the tension with objective measurement — the other half of Q3 (how to tell if you sleep well without a wearable).

## Content

### PSQI components — the seven dimensions of "sleeping well"

1. Perceived sleep quality (subjective rating)
2. Sleep latency (time to fall asleep)
3. Sleep duration (total hours)
4. Sleep efficiency (% of time in bed actually sleeping)
5. Sleep disturbances (nighttime awakenings)
6. Medication use
7. Daytime dysfunction

### Reliability

- **Internal consistency:** Cronbach's α = 0.76 (good)
- **Test-retest:** mean correlation 0.64 over 2–16 weeks (strong)
- **vs. polysomnography:** correlations are **surprisingly weak** (r between −0.33 and 0.52) — subjective and objective measures capture overlapping but distinct constructs

### Cutoffs

- **PSQI >5:** poor sleep quality (original threshold)
- **University students:** PSQI >6–7 optimal cutoff
- **Clinical insomnia detection:** PSQI >8.5

### Caveat — factor structure is inconsistent

Factor analyses across studies: 6 support unidimensional scoring, 6 find 2-factor models, 2 find 3-factor structures. Authors warn: "different factorial structures were found in a variety of samples, casting doubt on the usefulness of total score in detecting poor and good sleepers."

## Notable quotes

> Sleep quality is an important clinical construct since it is increasingly common for people to complain about poor sleep quality.

> Correlations between PSQI scores and actual polysomnography measurements were surprisingly weak.
